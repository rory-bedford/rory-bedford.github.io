I used to be a materialist. The success of science in transforming our world over the last 200 or so years has led many people to believe that it is the only sensible lens through which we can understand reality. We reduce the world to cold, lifeless matter, governed by deterministic or probabilistic laws.

Consider, for example, the biology of a living organism. To the scientist, this organism is a collection of distinct processes. At the smallest scale, you have a huge number of fundamental particles interacting with each other. Going up, we find enzymes and proteins, and then cells, all the way to the mechanics of muscles and bones. The age old question is therefore: what unites these distinct processes into a single organism? At first the biologist may point towards DNA, and sure, this is about the best answer that science can give. A dog is simply GTCACAATT..., its unique biological code, present in every cell in its body. However, this is ultimately still a physical definition, with pencilled-in boundaries.

Consider the Andromeda and Milky Way galaxies. What distinguishes one from the other? With a little knowledge of physics we could define Andromeda to be a collection of stars bound gravitationally to each other. However, the stars in the Milky Way also feel the gravitational pull of the stars in Andromeda. Are they not, therefore, a part of Andromeda too?

The point is, any such definition will always be arbitrary. It is obviously convenient to label one collection of stars as Andromeda and another the Milky Way, just as it is convenient to label one collection of cells Fido and another Rover. But these separations have no fundamental reality. They're just how we like to distinguish different bits of matter.

This is completely at odds with our experience of the world. Whether you admit it or not, you probably view yourself as more than an arbitrary collection of particles. You see your thoughts and feelings as more than just a series of distinct neurons firing. Is this merely an illusion, or is there perhaps more to the world than just matter?

To answer this question, I believe it is wise to take pointers from the scientific method. To figure out the nature of the world, scientists observe all the phenomena they possibly can. They poke and prod matter through a wide range of experiments to reveal its most interesting properties. Well, the same thing can be done with the mind. Meditation is nothing more than empiricism directed inward. And, just like the scientists, we should strive to approach this practise with a sense of curiosity, playfulness, and non-judgement, free from any bias or predispositions as to what we might find.

I believe that, just as these internal methods reflect those of the scientists, so too will the discoveries. The more we look inwards, the more we will see that our minds are just a collection of distinct processes. And as we realise this, the barriers that we draw at the edge of our minds will reveal themselves to be arbitrary. And just as the matter that makes up our body is constantly changing, so too is the qualia that makes up our mind. This is what I believe the Buddha meant by his doctrine of 'Anattā', or no-self.

Does this really mean that we, as individuals, don't exist? That our experience of ourselves is truly an illusion?

For pointers, let us return one final time to the material world. We have said that it is wrong to see physical objects as having their own reality or essence, distinguishing them from other physical objects. What, then, can be said about the material world? Must we follow this chain of separation all the way down to the bottom? Are fundamental particles the only things that can be said to have an essence?

Well, at the bottom, we find a most curious thing. In physics there is in fact no such thing as a distinct fundamental particle. It doesn't make sense to say 'This electron in this atom in some star in the Milky way is a different electron to this other electron in this star in Andromeda.'. In the wavefunction describing such a system, both electrons are so inextricably linked to each other that they can't be said to hold a distinct essence. And when we blow this up to include all particles in existence, all we find is a great big monster wavefunction under which every single particle is intertwined with every other particle. There is no separation, there is simply the whole.

So what then, can be said of our minds? What will we find as we follow the separation of all mental events to the bitter end? Are the boundaries we place at the edge of our minds forced to shrink inwards, so that each distinct thought or feeling has its own essence, reducing us as individuals to a collection of distinct parts?

I dont think so. What we will actually find is that the boundaries of our mind explode outwards. We become one with the whole, god-consciousness, connected to all.
